The Visual Family

Structure over prose — the kit of display fixtures that replace paragraphs with things you can scan

A museum doesn't hand you an essay

Walk into a good gallery and nobody hands you a wall of text. Instead you get fixtures: a pedestal under the important piece, a small wall label beside it, a glass case grouping related items, a diorama you can lean into. Your eyes scan — you understand the room before you've read a single full sentence.

Most fixtures need no electricity at all. A pedestal is just shaped wood. Only one or two have a switch — a spotlight you can click on. This module is the course's kit of fixtures: a family of structured visuals that turn paragraphs into things you scan.

🖼️
The opening secret

Not every element animates. The course's quiet weapon is this family of structured visuals that replace prose — file trees, cards, badges, step lists — and almost all of them are pure CSS, no moving parts.

Why this family exists at all

The course's content rule is blunt: every screen must be at least 50% visual. You can't hit that by writing prettier paragraphs — you hit it by reaching for a fixture instead. Knowing the family by name is also what lets you tell an AI exactly what you want.

💡
aha! — structure IS explanation

A good layout teaches before a single word is read. When you see three cards in a row, you already know "these are three peers." When you see a numbered ladder, you already know "this happens in order." The arrangement does the explaining — the words just confirm it.

Meet the fixtures — each with one job

Here's the whole family on one shelf. Each card is a fixture, and each "use it when…" line tells you the exact moment to reach for it. Five of these six are pure CSS — shaped wood, no wiring.

Boxes joined by arrows. Use it when "A then B then C" deserves to be seen, not read as a sentence.

A highlighted aside. Use it when there's an "aha!" insight or a warning that must not blend into the body text.

The one bit of wiring behind the click

That whole interaction is nine lines of JavaScript — and you never wrote an onclick on the boxes. The engine finds every box and wires it for you. Here's the exact handler, line by line.

CODE 
$$('.arch-component').forEach(comp => {
  comp.addEventListener('click', function () {
    const diagram = this.closest('.arch-diagram');
    $$('.arch-component', diagram).forEach(c => c.classList.remove('active'));
    this.classList.add('active');
    const descEl = $('.arch-description', diagram);
    if (descEl) descEl.textContent = this.dataset.desc || '';
  });
});
PLAIN ENGLISH

Find every box that's an "arch-component" and set each one up.

When this box is clicked, run the steps below.

Look upward from the clicked box to the nearest enclosing diagram. (That's what closest does — it scopes everything to this one diagram.)

Turn off the "active" highlight on every box in this diagram.

Now turn the highlight ON for the box you just clicked.

Grab the little description panel that belongs to this diagram.

Fill that panel with the note stored on the clicked box — its dataset.desc.

(closes the click handler)

(closes the setup loop — done for all boxes)
